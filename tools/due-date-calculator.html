Frequently Asked Questions

What is Naegele's Rule and how accurate is it?

Naegele's Rule is the standard obstetric method estimating delivery by adding 7 days to the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP) and subtracting 3 months. While it sets an excellent physiological baseline, it assumes a standard 28-day model. Only about 4% of entries deliver on the exact predicted date[cite: 15]. Delivery within two weeks before or after is highly standard and medically expected[cite: 15].

How many weeks long is a standard full-term pregnancy?

A standard clinical full-term pregnancy is calculated as approximately 280 days, which is equivalent to 40 weeks from the first day of your last menstrual period[cite: 15]. Your body works through three distinct trimesters, each bringing unique fetal growth phases and requiring specific nutritional focus areas.

Can my due date change during my doctor visits?

Yes, it is very common. Early calculations provide an estimate based on your cycle calendar. During your initial checkups, your obstetrician will measure the fetal Crown-Rump Length (CRL) using ultrasound imaging. If the ultrasound data differs from your cycle dates, your clinician will update your timeline to reflect the scan results.
