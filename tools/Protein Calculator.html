🔍 Frequently Asked Questions

What is the optimal daily protein intake for an average adult?

According to standard dietary guidelines, the Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for protein is roughly 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight for sedentary adults. However, this represents the minimum required to prevent deficiency. Individuals engaged in regular physical training, especially resistance exercises, typically require between 1.4 to 2.2 grams per kilogram to support muscle protein synthesis, recovery, and lean mass development.

Does consuming excess protein cause kidney damage?

Current clinical consensus and extensive nephrology research indicate that a high-protein diet does not inherently damage healthy kidneys. The human body efficiently processes and excretes excess amino acids. However, individuals with pre-existing renal conditions or chronic kidney disease (CKD) must strictly regulate their protein intake, as impaired kidneys struggle to filter the nitrogenous waste products generated by heavy protein metabolism.

How does age affect daily protein requirements?

As adults age, they naturally experience anabolic resistance, meaning their muscles become less responsive to the dietary protein signals that trigger muscle growth and maintenance. To combat sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss), older adults often need a higher baseline protein intake—frequently recommended between 1.0 to 1.2 grams per kilogram of body weight—distributed evenly across daily meals to maximize muscle preservation.
