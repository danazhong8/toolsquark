Why WHtR Often Outperforms BMI in Risk Analysis

While the traditional Body Mass Index (BMI) only checks the absolute relationship between your collective total weight and height, it completely fails to identify the location of body fat tissues. Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR) explicitly accounts for abdominal visceral adipose tissue—the harmful fat layer surrounding major internal organs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is WHtR considered better than BMI?+
BMI only measures total weight relative to height, ignoring fat distribution. WHtR directly measures abdominal fat accumulation, which is a far stronger clinical indicator of cardiovascular risk, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.
What is a healthy Waist-to-Height Ratio?+
As a general clinical rule of thumb, keeping your waist circumference to less than half your height (a ratio below 0.50) is associated with lower health risks for both men and women.
Where exactly should I measure my waist?+
Measure at the midpoint between the bottom of your lowest rib and the top of your hip bone (usually right across your belly button). Breathe out naturally and do not pull the tape too tight.
