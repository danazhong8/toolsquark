What This Calculator Produces

The calculator produces an estimated calorie target and converts it into grams of protein, fat, and carbohydrate using one fixed 30/35/35 energy split. This is a simple meal-planning preset, not an evidence-based optimum for every person.

Macro percentages can vary widely while still supporting a nutritious diet. Food quality, fiber, micronutrients, training demands, preferences, and adherence remain important.

Calculation Steps

BMR = Mifflin-St Jeor estimate
Target calories = BMR x activity factor, then -500 / 0 / +500 kcal by goal
Protein grams = target x 0.30 / 4
Fat grams = target x 0.35 / 9
Carbohydrate grams = target x 0.35 / 4

Protein and carbohydrate are converted at 4 kcal per gram and fat at 9 kcal per gram. Gram targets are rounded, so the reconstructed calorie total can differ slightly.

Worked Example

At a 2,000 kcal target, the preset allocates 600 kcal to protein, 700 kcal to fat, and 700 kcal to carbohydrate. That converts to 150 g protein, about 78 g fat, and 175 g carbohydrate.

How To Personalize The Starting Split

  • Protein: check whether the gram target is appropriate for body size, training, and medical context.
  • Fat: preserve enough dietary fat for food variety and essential nutrients.
  • Carbohydrate: adjust around training volume, tolerance, and preference after protein and fat needs are covered.

Judge the plan by adherence, hunger, digestion, performance, and multi-week weight trend rather than exact daily matching.

Limits And Safety

The calorie estimate inherits uncertainty from both the BMR equation and broad activity factors. The fixed split does not account for diabetes, kidney disease, pregnancy, breastfeeding, pediatric growth, eating-disorder history, allergies, or therapeutic diets.

The calculator places a 1,200 kcal floor on its fat-loss result, but that software limit does not establish that 1,200 kcal is suitable or safe for a particular person.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does this macro calculator work?

It estimates daily energy needs with the Mifflin-St Jeor equation and an activity multiplier, applies the selected goal adjustment, then uses a fixed planning split of 30% protein, 35% fat, and 35% carbohydrate.

Are these macros exact prescriptions?

No. They are practical planning estimates. Real needs vary with training, digestion, food preferences, adherence, and medical context.

Should I choose fat loss, maintenance, or muscle gain?

Choose fat loss for a planned calorie deficit, maintenance when you want weight stability, and muscle gain when you are pairing a calorie surplus with progressive resistance training.
